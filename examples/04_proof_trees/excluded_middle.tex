\section*{Law of Excluded Middle Examples}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Law of Excluded Middle Examples}

\subsection*{Example 1 : Statement of LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 1 : Statement of LEM}

\noindent The law of excluded middle states: for any proposition p, either p is true or $\lnot p$ is true.

\bigskip

\noindent
$p \lor \lnot p$


\noindent This is an axiom of classical logic but not of constructive logic.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 2 : Proof by Cases Using LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 2 : Proof by Cases Using LEM}

\noindent Prove: $(p \implies q) \implies ((\lnot p \implies q) \implies q)$

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{q}{
  \infer[\mbox{assumption}]{\lnot p \implies q}{\infer[LEM]{p \lor \lnot p}{\ulcorner p \implies q \urcorner^{[1]}} & \infer[\Rightarrow \mbox{elim with p} \Rightarrow q]{q}{} & \infer[\Rightarrow \mbox{elim with} \lnot p \Rightarrow q]{q}{} & \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{q}{\ulcorner p \implies q \urcorner^{[1]}}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent By LEM, we can case-analyze on p $\lor$ $\lnot p$. In both cases we derive q.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 3 : Double Negation Elimination Using LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 3 : Double Negation Elimination Using LEM}

\noindent Prove: $\lnot$ $\lnot p \implies p$

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{p}{
  \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{p}{\infer[false\textrm{-elim}]{p}{\infer[\mbox{contradiction with} \lnot \lnot p]{false}{}}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent LEM enables double negation elimination in classical logic.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 4 : De Morgan's Law Using LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 4 : De Morgan's Law Using LEM}

\noindent Prove: $\lnot$ $(p \land q)$ $\Rightarrow$ $(\lnot p \lor \lnot q)$

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{\lnot p \lor \lnot q}{
  \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{\lnot p \lor \lnot q}{\infer[\lor \mbox{intro left}]{\lnot p \lor \lnot q}{\infer[\mbox{identity}]{\lnot p}{}}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent This proof requires LEM to case-analyze on both p and q.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 5 : Decidability}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 5 : Decidability}

\noindent A proposition is decidable if we can prove p or not p. In logical notation, we would write: decidable(p) means $p(or)(\lnot p)$.

\bigskip

\noindent LEM states that all propositions are decidable in classical logic.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 6 : Proof by Contradiction via LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 6 : Proof by Contradiction via LEM}

\noindent Prove: $(\lnot p \implies false) \implies p$

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{p}{
  \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{p}{\infer[false\textrm{-elim}]{p}{\infer[\Rightarrow\textrm{-elim}]{false}{}}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent This shows that proof by contradiction follows from LEM.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 7 : Material Implication}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 7 : Material Implication}

\noindent Prove: $(p \implies q) \iff (\lnot p \lor q)$

\bigskip

\noindent Left-to-right $(p \implies (q \implies \lnot p \lor q))$:

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{\lnot p \lor q}{
  \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{\lnot p \lor q}{\infer[\lor \mbox{intro left}]{\lnot p \lor q}{}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent Right-to-left $(\lnot p \lor q \implies (p \implies q))$:

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\Rightarrow\textrm{-intro}^{[1]}]{p \implies q}{
  \infer[\mbox{assumption}]{p}{\infer[false\textrm{-elim}]{q}{\infer[\mbox{contradiction}]{false}{}} & \infer[\mbox{identity}]{q}{} & \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{q}{\ulcorner \lnot p \lor q \urcorner^{[1]}}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent Material implication equivalence uses LEM in the forward direction.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 8 : Pierce's Law}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 8 : Pierce's Law}

\noindent Prove: $((p \implies q) \implies p)$ $\Rightarrow$ p

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{p}{
  \infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{p}{\infer[\Rightarrow \mbox{elim with premise}]{p}{\infer[\Rightarrow\textrm{-intro}^{[2]}]{p \implies q}{\infer[false\textrm{-elim}]{q}{\infer[\mbox{contradiction}]{false}{\ulcorner p \urcorner^{[2]}}}}}}
}
$%
}
\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent Pierce's law is equivalent to LEM in classical logic.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 9 : Tertium Non Datur}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 9 : Tertium Non Datur}

\noindent Tertium non datur (Latin: "no third possibility") is another name for LEM.

\bigskip

\noindent For any proposition p, there is no third option besides p and $\lnot p$.

\bigskip

\noindent
$\forall p @ p \lor \lnot p$


\noindent This principle distinguishes classical from intuitionistic logic.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 10 : Constructive vs Classical}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 10 : Constructive vs Classical}

\noindent In constructive (intuitionistic) logic, LEM is not provable as a general principle.

\bigskip

\noindent Constructive: To prove p or not p, you must either: - Construct a proof of p, or - Construct a proof of not p (a proof that p leads to contradiction)

\bigskip

\noindent Classical: LEM is an axiom—every proposition is assumed decidable without construction.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 11 : Example Where LEM Is Not Needed}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 11 : Example Where LEM Is Not Needed}

\noindent Some propositions are constructively decidable:

\bigskip

\noindent
$\forall n : \nat @ (n = 0) \lor \lnot (n = 0)$


\noindent We can decide this by checking if n equals 0.

\bigskip

\noindent But general propositions may not be decidable constructively:

\bigskip

\noindent
$\forall p @ p \lor \lnot (p - -(requires(LEM))) \in general(case)$


\subsection*{Example 12 : Proof Strategy with LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 12 : Proof Strategy with LEM}

\noindent Common proof strategy using LEM:

\bigskip

\noindent To prove Q: 1. Invoke p or $\lnot p$ for some relevant p 2. [case] p prove Q assuming p 3. [case] $\lnot p$ prove Q assuming $\lnot p$ 4. Conclude Q by or elim

\bigskip

\noindent This technique is called "proof by cases" or "case analysis."

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 13 : LEM land Indirect Proof}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 13 : LEM land Indirect Proof}

\noindent Indirect proof pattern using LEM:

\bigskip

\noindent To prove p: 1. Assume not p [assumption 1] 2. Derive false 3. Conclude $\lnot$ $\lnot p$ [$\lnot$ intro from 1] 4. Apply LEM: p $\lor$ $\lnot p$ 5. [case] p p 6. [case] $\lnot p$ contradicts $\lnot$ $\lnot p$, derive p by false elim 7. Conclude p [or elim]

\bigskip

\noindent LEM enables the final step (double negation elimination).

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 14 : Drinker Paradox}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 14 : Drinker Paradox}

\noindent Prove: There exists a person x such that if x drinks, then everyone drinks.

\bigskip

\begin{center}
\adjustbox{max width=\textwidth}{%
$\displaystyle
\infer[\lor\textrm{-elim}]{\exists x @ x(drinks) \implies (\forall y @ y(drinks))}{

  a
}}}}} & \infer[\exists \mbox{intro with x}=b]{\exists x @ x(drinks) \implies (\forall y @ y(drinks))}{\infer[\Rightarrow\textrm{-intro}^{[2]}]{b(drinks) \implies (\forall y @ y(drinks))}{\infer[\mbox{assumption}]{b(drinks)}{\infer[\exists\textrm{-elim}]{\lnot ((b(drinks)))}{\infer[\mbox{negation of} \forall]{\exists b @ \lnot ((b(drinks)))}{}}}}}}

$%

\end{center}
\bigskip

\noindent This classical proof uses LEM crucially.

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 15 : When to Use LEM}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 15 : When to Use LEM}

\noindent Use LEM when: 1. You need to prove something by case analysis on an arbitrary proposition 2. You're working in classical logic (not constructive) 3. You need double negation elimination 4. Your proof strategy requires considering both p $\land$ $\lnot p$

\bigskip

\noindent Avoid LEM when: 1. Working in constructive/intuitionistic logic 2. The proposition has a decidable, constructive proof 3. You want algorithms that compute witnesses (constructive proofs)

\bigskip

\subsection*{Example 16 : Relationship to Other Principles}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Example 16 : Relationship to Other Principles}

\noindent In classical logic, these are equivalent: - Law of excluded middle: p $\lor$ $\lnot p$ - Double negation elimination: $\lnot$ $\lnot p$ $\Rightarrow$ p - Pierce's law: $((p \implies q) \implies p)$ $\Rightarrow$ p - Reductio ad absurdum: $(\lnot p \implies false)$ $\Rightarrow$ p

\bigskip

\noindent Accepting any one of these principles gives you classical logic.

\bigskip

\noindent In intuitionistic logic, none of these hold in general—you must construct proofs explicitly.

\bigskip
